NPR spoke with Ty Malugani, a devoted soccer fan in Alabama, about why he — and many others who had long dreamed of attending a World Cup on home soil — ended up sitting this one out.

---

**NPR:** Were you excited when the World Cup was announced for the US?

**Ty Malugani:** I was so excited. We always talked about going to a World Cup, and so to be like, oh, there's one in America, that's perfect, perfect timing.

---

**NPR:** What changed?

**Ty Malugani:** ==Malugani described a frustrating process: not a lottery for tickets, but a lottery for a time slot just to attempt purchasing them.== Even the cheapest seats to a U.S. group stage match — high up in the upper deck — came out to over $1,500 for a family with four young children, before factoring in travel or accommodation.
