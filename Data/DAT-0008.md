NPR's Jasmine Garsd spent time in the borough of Queens, along the 7 train — the subway line known as the International Express, where every stop is a different immigrant enclave — to hear how locals are living the World Cup being played about 20 miles away.

---

**Guillermo Andrade** (soccer coach, Flushing Meadows Park; migrated from Peru 25 years ago): "I'd hand out flyers on the street in Manhattan. It was winter. It was cold. And I would ask myself, what am I even doing here? And then one day, I took the 7 line out to Queens... Five days a week, I felt dead. But on weekends, on these fields, I came to life."

**NPR (Jasmine Garsd):** Andrade is excited to see the matches, especially Cristiano Ronaldo, who is purportedly playing his last cup. But he won't be going to any actual matches. With the cheapest ticket at around a thousand dollars, the World Cup is going to be a distant event for most in this working-class community — something to be watched in restaurants and bars. Andrade predicts many will stay home out of caution: several of his players have recently been picked up by ICE.

---

**Elodia** (soccer jersey vendor near Corona Station, in her 70s; asked to withhold her last name for fear of being targeted by ICE agents): "I think it's hypocritical. The U.S. is going to make a lot of money off of this, and meanwhile, we're being treated like animals."

**NPR (Jasmine Garsd):** ICE officials have said they won't be planning any enforcement activity at World Cup matches. But Elodia says a lot of people are going to be too scared to come out and watch.

---

**NPR (Jasmine Garsd):** Still, there is a great deal of soccer joy out here. Near the 90th Street station, dozens stand in the shade trading Panini World Cup stickers. Among them, 13-year-old Juan David Iza, a die-hard Ecuador fan, opening fresh sticker packets over a lunch of salchipapas.

---

**Angel Ojeda** (fan sitting under a giant Lionel Messi mural in Little Argentina, wearing his Argentina jersey): "We're going to repeat as champions. I have no doubts. I could be wrong. No, I don't think I am."

---

**Shipud Costa** (customer service worker at LaGuardia Airport, Little Bangladesh): "First, I run and hug. I have to. I break all the securities... My advice to Messi — so he can take the Q70 free bus."

**NPR (Jasmine Garsd):** Bangladesh did not qualify, but Costa dreams of Messi flying into LaGuardia one day — take the free Q70 bus, transfer to the 7 line, and welcome to Queens.
